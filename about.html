My Journey to Tech

For years, I was the person people called when something new needed to be built — a community project, a youth leadership program, or an organization from the ground up. I’ve been a social entrepreneur, a tour guide, a teacher, a founder, and a father. Most of my adult life has been spent in spaces where human complexity is the norm, and easy answers are rare.

So why am I learning to code?

The shift wasn’t sudden, but it became urgent. After October 7th, like many others in this region, I found the ground beneath me changed. The work that had always felt essential—helping communities navigate conflict—no longer felt sustainable, at least not in the same way. I was supporting others while struggling to support my own family. I needed stability. But I wasn’t ready to abandon meaning.

That’s when I started to look at technology—not as an escape, but as a tool. Tech can build systems. It can scale good ideas. It can connect people across boundaries. And unlike peace-building, which often lives in moments and conversations, tech leaves artifacts—codebases, platforms, tools—that can outlast us.

At first, it was intimidating. I’m used to text—Torah, political philosophy, policy briefs—not text editors. I’ve facilitated negotiations, but the first time I typed git push origin main, I was just hoping I didn’t break the internet.

And over time, something familiar emerged. Clean code is a form of empathy. Building a backend is another way of structuring relationships. Frontend logic is storytelling. And debugging? That’s just conflict resolution in disguise.

Today, I’m a full-stack developer in training. I’m building my skills in React, Node.js, and Python — not to escape the world I came from, but to bring its values with me into new spaces. Whether I’m designing an app or contributing to an open-source tool, I’m still doing what I’ve always done: listening deeply, making connections, and helping people build something better together.

didn’t lose my passion for building community—I just changed my tools. And as I grow as a developer, I’m exploring where meaningful tech can support real-world connection, access, and impact.

This is my pivot. It’s not a departure. It’s a translation.
